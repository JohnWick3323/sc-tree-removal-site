## **What Makes a Tree Hazardous?**

Not every tree that looks concerning is genuinely hazardous, and not every hazardous tree looks obviously dangerous. Identifying the specific conditions that make a tree a genuine structural threat requires experience, a trained eye, and a thorough understanding of how trees behave under stress — particularly in South Carolina's demanding climate and storm environment.

South Carolina Tree Removal Experts has been assessing hazardous trees across this state for 38 years. Below are the most common and most serious conditions that classify a tree as hazardous and warrant professional removal.

### **A Significant or Worsening Lean Toward a Structure**

A tree that has developed a noticeable lean — particularly one that appeared or worsened suddenly after a storm — is one of the most serious hazardous tree situations a South Carolina homeowner can face. 

A leaning tree places enormous and uneven stress on one side of the root system, and that root system may already be partially failed or significantly weakened without showing any visible signs above ground.

The critical detail to watch for is not just the lean itself but any changes at the base of the tree. Soil cracking or lifting on one side, exposed roots pulling out of the ground, or a visible gap forming between the root ball and the surrounding earth are all signs that the tree is in the process of uprooting — and that full failure could be imminent, particularly during South Carolina's hurricane season or a powerful summer thunderstorm.

### **A Split, Cracked, or Co-Dominant Trunk**

A trunk that has developed a deep vertical crack, a visible split running through a major section of wood, or a co-dominant stem structure — where two major trunks of roughly equal size grow from the same base — is a serious structural hazard. 

These conditions create weak points in the tree's primary load-bearing structure, making it highly vulnerable to failure under wind loading, ice accumulation, or the weight of a saturated canopy after heavy South Carolina rainfall.

Co-dominant stems are particularly common in species like Sweetgum and Tulip Poplar, and the V-shaped junction where they meet is one of the most structurally weak points found in any tree. When that junction begins to show cracking, bark inclusion, or visible separation, the tree has reached a point where professional assessment and likely removal is the only responsible course of action.

### **Major Root Damage or Root System Compromise**

The root system is the foundation of every tree, and when it is compromised, the entire structure above it becomes unstable. Root damage in South Carolina is more common than many homeowners realize — construction activity, soil compaction from heavy equipment, trenching for utilities, prolonged soil saturation after heavy rainfall, and the natural aging of large trees all contribute to root system deterioration, significantly increasing failure risk.

Above-ground signs of root damage include visible root heaving at the soil surface, sections of ground that feel soft or spongy near the base of the tree, mushroom or fungal growth along the root zone, and a tree that has begun to lean without an obvious above-ground cause. 

Southern Live Oaks and Water Oaks with compromised root systems are among the most dangerous hazardous tree situations our crew encounters across the Charleston, Summerville, and Columbia areas — because their sheer size and canopy weight mean a root failure can cause a catastrophic, sudden collapse.

### **Large Dead Sections Within a Living Tree**

A tree does not need to be entirely dead to be hazardous. Large dead branches or major dead sections within the canopy of an otherwise living tree — sometimes called widow makers — represent one of the most underestimated hazards on residential properties across South Carolina.

These dead sections rapidly lose their moisture content and structural flexibility, becoming heavy, brittle, and prone to sudden failure under wind loading or the weight of accumulated ice during one of South Carolina's periodic winter ice storms. 

A large dead limb hanging over a roof, a driveway, a vehicle, or a children's play area is an active danger that warrants immediate professional attention — regardless of how healthy the rest of the tree appears to be.

### **Trees Overhanging Structures, Vehicles, or Power Lines**

Location is one of the most important factors in determining whether a tree is hazardous. A perfectly healthy tree that has grown to the point where its canopy is directly overhanging your roof, its major limbs are making contact with your home's exterior, or its branches have grown into or near overhead power lines presents a serious and ongoing hazard that only increases with every passing storm season.

Trees in contact with or growing near power lines create both a fire risk and an electrocution hazard that requires professional intervention — not a DIY trimming attempt. Trees overhanging structures concentrate storm damage risk directly onto your home, because every wind event or ice accumulation in that canopy transfers its destructive force to the structure below. South Carolina Tree Removal Experts assesses proximity-based hazards as part of every free estimate, giving you a clear and honest picture of the risk level before any decisions are made.

### **Storm Damage That Left the Tree Structurally Compromised**

South Carolina's storm seasons — from the Atlantic hurricane season, running June through November, to the powerful summer thunderstorm pattern and periodic winter ice events — regularly leave trees in a state of structural compromise that is not always obvious to a casual visual inspection. 

A tree that survived a recent storm with what appears to be minor branch loss may have sustained significant trunk cracking, major root system disruption, or deep canopy damage that dramatically increases its failure risk in the next weather event.

Storm-damaged trees are particularly unpredictable because the stress and structural compromise from one weather event weaken the tree's ability to withstand the next. Loblolly Pines, Sweetgums, and Tulip Poplars across Greenville, Spartanburg, Myrtle Beach, and Florence are among the species and areas our crew most frequently responds to after major storm events — because these species and these communities experience significant storm-related tree damage on a regular basis throughout the year.

The repair costs from a direct tree strike on a South Carolina home routinely run into tens of thousands of dollars, and in severe cases, the damage can make a home temporarily uninhabitable.

The destruction rarely stays contained to the immediate impact zone either. Falling trees take out gutters, shatter windows, bring down power lines, and destroy landscaping — creating layers of secondary damage that significantly compound the original loss.

A hazardous tree that is addressed while it still has some structural integrity remaining is a far more manageable removal than one that has been left to deteriorate to the point of near-collapse. 

As a tree's structural condition worsens, the unpredictability of the removal process increases — compromised wood behaves differently under cutting equipment, failed root systems make the tree more unstable during the operation, and trees that have already begun to fall and are hung up on structures or other trees create complex and dangerous rigging situations.
